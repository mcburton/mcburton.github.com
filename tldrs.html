Summary of "Derrida: The Excluded Favorite by Emily Eakin" (via tldr.io)

  • Deconstruction posits "every structure...that organizes our experience is constituted and maintained through acts of exclusion.” & the excluded always come back
  • Derrida's insights into exclusion are rooted in experiences like the racism of his youth and his denial of professorship in Paris
  • "Americans, it’s been said, often got Derrida wrong: deconstruction was never intended as a reproducible methodology, let alone a political weapon."
  • Peeters casts Derrida as "perennial outsider," and in his words “has refused to exclude anything” resulting in a "surfeit of equivalency" wrt Derrida's life
  • Peeter's posits "the underlying intuition—that in Derrida’s abstractions was a powerful story about the experience of being shut out and unheard" are sound

Summary of "Scholarship: Beyond the paper" (via tldr.io)

  • The web is transforming scholarly communication, erasing the artificial distinction between process and product. Peer-review does not scale with the web.
  • Peer-review's filtering function will be replaced by algorithms that distill community impact; PageRank-like altmetrics are a new "decoupled" measure of impact
  • 'decoupled journals' embrace open, interoperable web services & standards. They filter/review though continuous, not punctuated, flows of evaluation
  • Reward structures WILL evolve to recognize new metrics & scholarly products. What to do? Publish new web-native products & take risks w/ web-publishing.
  • "scholarship has always been a community enterprise" & that community is online. Let's leverage the web's affordances to publish, share, & evaluate new products

Summary of "Discovering Scholarship on the Open Web: Communities and Methods" (via tldr.io)

  • "This report outlines the current state of the aggregation, curation, evaluation, and distribution of scholarship on the open web." especially "gray lit"
  • Finding quality gray lit dispersed across the web is challenging, hard to "to identify, evaluate, collect, and highlight."
  • Four categories of content management on the open web: Repositories (arXive), aggregators (techmeme), post-pub peer-review (F1000), curated content (DHNow)
  • quality can be determined via features of the content or features of its use. Editorial oversight is still crucial to selection. challenge: how to automate
  • Article outlines an 8 point rubric for evaluating scholarly comm on the open web. claims: we need better FOSS tools to assist editors for managing open content
